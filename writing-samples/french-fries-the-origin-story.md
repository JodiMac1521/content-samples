# French Fries: America's Favorite Side Dish

**By Jodi McPherson, 6th Grade**
*The piece that started everything.*

---

If you have ever wondered why french fries taste so good, you are not alone. Scientists, chefs, and regular people have been asking this same question for hundreds of years. The answer is more interesting than you might think.

French fries are not actually from France. Most historians believe they were invented in Belgium, where people have been frying potatoes in oil since the late 1600s. American soldiers discovered them during World War I and called them "french fries" because the Belgian soldiers they met spoke French. This is what you call a historical misunderstanding, but it is one that everybody is okay with.

The most important part of a good french fry is the potato. Not just any potato will do. The Russet Burbank potato is the most popular choice because it has a lot of starch and not very much water. When you put a Russet in hot oil, the outside gets crispy and golden while the inside stays soft and fluffy. If you use the wrong potato, the whole thing turns out soggy, which is a disappointment.

The oil temperature also matters a lot. The best french fries are actually cooked twice. First at a lower temperature, around 325 degrees, to cook the inside. Then at a higher temperature, around 375 degrees, to make the outside crispy. McDonald's does something close to this, which is probably why their fries taste the way they do.

I think what makes french fries so special is that they are simple but they are also not simple at all. They are just potatoes and oil and salt. But if you change anything — the potato, the oil, the temperature, or even how long you wait before you eat them — they become a completely different food. My mom always says that the best fries are eaten immediately, standing up, before you even get to the table. I think she is right about this.

The next time someone tells you that french fries are junk food, you can tell them they are actually a very complicated subject.

## Why This Piece Lives Here

This is the origin story.

Long before messaging hierarchy and buyer's journey frameworks, before ghostwriting executive thought leadership and positioning SaaS companies, there was an 11-year-old in 6th grade who couldn't just *eat* the fries. She had to understand them. Research them. Find the angle no one was talking about — that they aren't actually French, that simplicity is deceptive, that the best version of anything requires conditions most people never think about.

That instinct — **curiosity before conclusion** — is the throughline in every piece of writing that followed.

The research-first impulse became UX methodology.
The "simple but not simple at all" framing became messaging architecture.
The unexpected historical turn became the antithesis POV.
